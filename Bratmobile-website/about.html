About Bratmobile

Allsion Wolfe

Wolfe, born november 9, 1969 is a Los Angeles- based singer, songwriter, writer, and podcaster. She is one of the founding members and the lead singer of the punk rock band Bratmobile, she is also one of the leading voices of the riot grrrl movement.

Wolfe and her sister Cindy were born identical twins and grew up in Olympia, Washington. Their parents divorced when they were still young children, and they were raised by their mother, Pat Shively.

A radical feminist and self-described lesbian, Shiverly founded Olympia’s Eastside Women’s Health Clinic in 1981.

it was the first women’s clinic in Thurston County, and throughout Shively’s two decades of work it was the target of relentless anti-abortion demostrations.

Molly Neuman

Neuman, born June 1972, is an American drummer, writer and publisher, originally from the Washington, D.C. area who has performed in the band Bratmobile and in two other bands which are the Frumpues, and the PeeChees.

She was a pioneer of the early-to-mid ‘90s riot grrrl movement, penning the zine which coined the phrase in its title.

She also co-wrote Girl Germs with Bratmobile singer Allinson Wolfe while the two were students at the University of Oregon that title later became the name of a bramobile song.

Erin Smith

Erin Smith, born August 16, 1972, in Washington, D.C., is best known for being the guitarist in Bratmobile.

Smith started her zine Teenage Gang Debs in 1987 with her brother Don. In 1991, she started interning at Sassy magazine, where she wrote articles about independent music and exposed girls to DIY culture.

Also in 1991, she began to play guitar for Bratmobile with Molly Neuman on the drums and Allison Wolfe on vocals.

Origins

It all began with Allison Wolfe and Molly Neuman trying to get out of a gig that Wolfe’s friend Calvin Johnson, an indie musician who also co-owned K Records, had asked them to do in Olympia on Valentine’s Day in 1991.

Although Wolfie knew and admitted that they were a so-called “Fake band” since they didn't play any instruments then they had still written some songs which they performed a cappella with. They ended up agreeing to seek the help of Some Velvet Sidewalk member Robert Christie who let them borrow rehearsal space and equipment.

Here Robert advised them to listen to some Ramones for inspiration where in response to the advice they got Wolfe said, “Something in me clicked. Like, okay if most boy punk rock bands just listen to the Ramones and that’s how they write their songs, then we’ll do the opposite and I won’t listen to any Ramones and that way we’ll sound different.”

And that's how with five original songs, the band played its first show as a two-woman act at Olumpia’s North Shore Surf Club on February 14, 1991 with Neuman and Wolfe sharing duties on guitar, drums and vocals. The very next day they recorded the first Kill Rock Stars compilation, which was released later that year.

Riot grrrl

The riot grrrl movement originated in the early 1990s, when a group of women from Olympia, Washington, held a meeting about sexism in their local punk scenes. They intentionally used the word “girl” in order to focus on childhood, which is a time where children have the strongest self-esteem and belief in themselves.

Riot grrrls then took a growling “R”, replacing the “I” in the word as a way to take back the derogatory use of the term.

The Riot grrrl musicians/to-be where inspired by the musical drama movie Ladies and Gentlemen, The Fabulous Stains, which tells the story of a fictional seemingly proto-riot grrrl band.

Bratmobile was a first-generation riot grrrl band that became the second-most prominent founding voice of the riot grrrl movement.

In 1990, University of Oregon students Allison Wolfe and Molly Neuman collaborated on feminist zine Girl Germs with Washington DC’s Jen Smith, touching on sexism in their local music scenes.

What is Riot grrrl?

Because we girls want to create mediums that speak to US. We are tired of boy band after boy band, boy zine, boy punk after boy punk after boy… Because we need to talk to each other.

Communication and inclusion are key. We will never know if we don't break the code of silence… Because in every form of media we see ourselves slapped, decapitated, laughed at, objectified, raped, tricialized, pushed, ignored, stereotyped, kicked, scorned, molested, silenced, invalidated, knifed, shot, choked and killed.

Because a safe space needs to be created for girls where we can open our eyes and reach out to each other without being threatened by this sexist society and our day to day bullshit.
